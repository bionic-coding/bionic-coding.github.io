## Headlines

**1. Ship one timely article this week — and the Fable story just got its hook.**
`garden-news-ship-timely-article` · The Articles queue is four outline-only drafts,
zero published, and it's the most perishable content you have. The news pass turned
up the piece that changes the odds: Claude Fable 5 (announced June 9) was
**suspended on June 12 by a US export-control directive and restored in July** —
that suspension-and-restoration is exactly the "why the relaunch happened" your
*Fable, Relaunched* draft leaves as a TODO, and it's a genuinely striking,
human-interest story for a non-technical reader (a model you were using can vanish
by government order). GPT-5.6 "Sol" also reached general availability July 9, so the
head-to-head draft can finally cite real dates, prices, and benchmarks. My vote:
finish *Fable, Relaunched* first — it has a hook the others don't, and hooks decay.
I dropped a sourced fact sheet in your inbox to feed it (see Artifacts).

**2. The "cite through crux" promise is unkept — the pipeline has never run for a lesson or article.**
`garden-improvement-crux-sourcing-pipeline` · Every article draft and the in-progress
lesson ends with the same line: "Route through crux; cite the source page." It's the
spine of your content brief — facts and references, no unsourced claims. But the
research wiki holds exactly one source (the manifesto); not one lesson or article
yet carries a crux-sourced citation. This is the gap I'd close first structurally,
because it's what makes the teaching trustworthy and it compounds: every source you
ingest for an article is one the lessons can reuse. The inbox drop is a deliberate
first exercise of that pipeline — dispatch it through `ingest-research` and you've
run the loop once for real.

**3. "Durable intent, disposable code" is also your content policy.**
`garden-idea-durable-disposable-content` · The manifesto's thesis maps cleanly onto
the two content tracks, and naming that out loud settles the brief's biggest open
question (the freshness mechanics). **Lessons are the durable asset** — evergreen,
sourced, carrying a "Last reviewed" date and a weekly freshness pass. **Articles are
disposable by design** — timely, perishable, stamped "as of <date>," and *allowed*
to go stale rather than endlessly maintained. That framing gives you a rule instead
of a chore: Articles get an explicit as-of date and an expiry expectation; Lessons
get the review cadence. The weekly freshness pass is a natural thing to hand me on a
future night — I can flag lessons whose `updated:` has aged and whose claims the news
has moved past.
