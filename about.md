We're living through a shift I expect to prove bigger than the industrial revolution. Machines can now take over the *making* of things we assumed only people could make — software, most of all. The interesting question isn't whether they can. It's whether the human stays in control while they do.

That question is why this site is called what it is. **Bionic, not automatic.** *Bionic* means a person made more capable — augmented, still deciding, hands on the wheel — not a person replaced. The machine takes over the manufacturing; you keep the judgment it can't supply: what must be true, what must never break, and how you'd know. The augmentation runs the opposite direction from autocomplete — it doesn't write your code for you, it hands you the leverage and leaves you in charge.

That's the posture behind everything here. The easy thing to do with these tools is let go of the wheel — spray output at the world and hope. This site pulls the other way: toward keeping a human firmly in the loop, and treating AI-assisted work as **engineering**, not slop. That thread ties together the four things this site is.

- **[The Manifesto]({{ "/manifesto/" | relative_url }})** — the reason this site exists. One claim about software: **code is now disposable**, and we have to rethink how we build once that's true. It's my current thinking, written to be argued with — not a finished doctrine.
- **[Lessons]({{ "/learn/" | relative_url }})** — a plain-language field guide to modern AI, for people who want to understand the pieces without building models for a living. Start anywhere.
- **[Articles]({{ "/articles/" | relative_url }})** — thinking out loud: notes on what's new, a weekly read on the news, and whatever I'm working through.
- **[Tools]({{ "/tools/" | relative_url }})** — things I've built, and things I recommend, for doing this work the careful way.

## The manifesto, in a line

For fifty years we treated source code as the asset. It never was. The durable thing is *intent* — the invariants a system must honor, and the ability to regenerate the code from them on demand, cheaply and provably. The human owns those invariants; the machine owns the manufacturing. That's the line the whole method draws, and it's where control stays with you.
